You’re referring to a very recent discovery — the so-called “Inocle” extrachromosomal DNA element — so I’ll first summarise what the research team found, and then compare **how much genetic information it contains** relative to typical DNA (e.g., chromosomes/plasmids) and point out key caveats.

---

### ✅ What we *know* about Inocles

Here’s what the study by Yuya Kiguchi and colleagues (published in *Nature Communications) reports:

* They developed a saliva sample protocol (called *preNuc*) to remove most of the human DNA and then used long‐read metagenomic sequencing to detect large circular DNA elements in bacterial cells in human saliva. ([PMC][1])
* These large circular DNA elements they called “Inocles” (insertion sequence-encoded; oral origin; circle genomic structure).
* Characteristically: average size approx **~350-395 kilobase pairs (kbp)** (i.e., ~0.35 – 0.4 Mb) for those they closed. ([PubMed][2])
* They found these in about 74 % of the ~476 saliva metagenomes they surveyed. ([PMC][1])
* The candidate host genus is a common oral bacterium: Streptococcus salivarius (among others) in the oral cavity. ([PMC][1])
* Functionally, the Inocle elements appear to carry genes related to stress tolerance (oxidative stress, DNA damage repair), cell‐wall biosynthesis/modification (thus potential interaction with host cells) and appear to correlate with human immune cell/protein levels, and in some cases show reduced abundance in certain cancer patients (head/neck + colorectal). ([PubMed][2])

So yes: the discovery is legitimate (peer‐reviewed) and the transcript you provided corresponds quite well with the key findings.

---

### 📏 How much genetic information does an Inocle carry vs typical DNA?

Here’s a comparative snapshot:

| Genetic element                    | Typical size / genetic “information load”                                    | Inocle size  | Remarks                                                                                                                       |
| ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Bacterial chromosome (single cell) | Often **~1–10 Megabase pairs (Mb)** for many bacteria (e.g., ~2–5 Mb common) | –            | The main “blueprint” of the organism.                                                                                         |
| Typical bacterial plasmid          | Often tens of kb up to <100 kb (some larger but rare)                        | –            | Accessory genes, mobile. The paper says: “plasmids are at most a few tens of kb” in many cases. ([New Atlas][3])              |
| Inocle                             | ~350-400 kb (i.e., about 0.35–0.4 Mb)                                        | ~0.35–0.4 Mb | So it’s ~3-10× larger than typical accessory plasmids, but still much smaller than a full bacterial chromosome in many cases. |

So to put it in simple terms:

* If a typical plasmid is maybe ~10-50 kb, then an Inocle at ~350 kb is **~7-35 times bigger**.
* But compared to a bacterial chromosome of e.g. 2 Mb, the Inocle is still perhaps only ~15-20% of that size (or less).
* In human genome terms (the human haploid genome ~3,200 Mb or 3.2 Gb), the Inocle is utterly tiny in comparison (orders of magnitude smaller).

Thus: the amount of “genetic information” that an Inocle carries is **substantial for an extrachromosomal element**, but **modest compared to full chromosomes**.

---

### 🧐 Important caveats & context

* Just because it’s ~350 kb doesn’t mean *all* of that is functional or uniquely “genetic information” in the sense of coding genes. The study reports that only ~5 % of the genes they found in Inocles could be annotated (i.e., ~95 % remain uncharacterised). ([PMC][1])
* The “information load” isn’t strictly proportional to size: some DNA may be repetitive, non‐coding, regulatory; we don’t yet know how many of these genes are essential or what their expression/impact is.
* This is an extrachromosomal element inside bacteria residing *in* the human mouth; it’s not “human DNA” per se (i.e., the human genome), but rather part of microbial (bacterial) genomes and a mobile accessory.
* The functional impact on *human health* is speculative at this point. The correlation with immune markers and cancer patients is intriguing but not yet causal.
* The fact that standard sequencing missed these before reminds us that our knowledge of microbial “dark matter” is still expanding; we don’t yet know the full diversity or the full “information output” of such elements.

---

### 🔍 Answering your original question:

> *“How much genetic information is contained in inocles compared to what most people consider DNA?”*

Here’s how I’d phrase it for clarity:

* If by “what most people consider DNA” we mean the main genome (e.g., the chromosome of a bacterium, or in humans the full genome), then Inocles contain **far less** information than a full genome/chromosome.
* If by “what most people consider DNA” we mean common plasmids (mobile pieces of DNA in bacteria), then Inocles contain **much more** information — several times the size of many plasmids.
* Numerically: ~350-400 kb carried by Inocles vs e.g. ~10-50 kb for a typical small plasmid; vs ~1-5 Mb (or more) for a bacterial chromosome; vs ~3,000 Mb for human haploid genome.
* So the Inocles represent a significantly large “library” of genes for bacteria in the oral microbiome, but they are **not** on the scale of a full organism’s genome.
